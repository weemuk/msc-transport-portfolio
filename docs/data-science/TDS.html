A spatial analysis of the 20-minute city strategy in Cambridge

1 Introduction

1.1 Background and context

In 2023, Cambridgeshire County Council established an active travel strategy that aim to achieve net zero carbon emissions by 2045 (Cambridgeshire County Council, 2023). The strategy aligns with the 15-minute city framework, where cities need to ‘adopt urban planning mechanisms to ensure that quality of life is preserved’ (Moreno et al., 2021, 97) Therefore, the strategy has the goal to develop compact, dense and diverse neighbourhoods where residents can access the essential opportunities (such as healthcare, education, amenities and leisure) by active travel and public transport modes (Cervero and Kockelman, 1997). Cambridge has adopted these concepts to its specific spatial and socio-economic context and set a target to become a 20-minute active travel city.

1.2 Aims and objectives

This report aims to evaluate the spatial and socio-economic equity impacts of the 20-minute active travel strategy within the urban and peri-urban areas of Cambridge (Cambridgeshire County Council, 2023). Therefore, this study adopts a 20-minute approach for the spatial accessibility analysis to examine the following objectives:

  • Develop a multimodal routing engine: Using the r5r package to build a detailed network system to measure the accessibility across three different modes: walking, cycling and public transport.

  • Identify the inacessibility areas: Isolate the areas where residents cannot reach essential Points of Interest (POIs), such as healthcare, education, amenities and leisure; therefore, analyse the accessibility scores that fall bellow a defined essential threshold.

  • Evaluate the social exclusion: Analyse the correlation between the aggregated transport accessibility and the social exclusion from the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) for the Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) boundaries.

1.3 Research Questions

To address the aims and objectives defined above, this report is structured to answer the following three primary research questions:

RQ01 - Spatial accessibility: How does the 20-minute accessibility to essential POIs (healthcare, education, amenities and leisure) vary accross Cambridge while comparing walking, cycling and public transport modes in the city?

RQ02 - Spatial exclusion: Where are the primary inaccessible areas located? And why do these ‘transport deserts’ areas fail to provide proper accessibility within the 20-minute threshold to the residents?

RQ03 - Socio-economic equity: To what extent does the transport spatial and social exclusion affect the most vulnerable residents, analysed at the LSOA level?

2.2 Definition of the study area

The study area for the scope of this report is defined as the urban and peri-urban areas of Cambridge to analise the accessibility of the 20-minute active travel strategy in the city. Then, the proper study area is defined as the LSOAs that rely within a 5km buffer around the geometric centre of Cambridge to capture the areas that are within a reasonable distance from the city centre, and where most of the population and essential services are located.

To execute a high-resolution accessibility analysis using the r5r package, a hexagonal grid with a cell size of 100 meters was created across the study area (Transport for London, 2015). The centroids of these hexagons were used as the origins for the accessibility analysis, providing a detailed spatial representation of accessibility levels across Cambridge.

3 Methodology

3.1 High-resolution routing - r5r

To calculate the 20-minute accessibility across Cambridge, this study used the r5r package (Pereira et al., 2021), which provides a multimodal routing engine through the r5r::build_network() function that can integrate spatial datasets, such as GTFS for public transport, elevation DEM data for active travel modes, and detailed OSM built network. Moreover, the r5r package allowed the calculation of accessibility metrics, through the r5r::accessibility() function, based on travel time thresholds, essential tool for the present study. The following steps outline the methodology for setting up the routing engine and calculating accessibility scores:

# Set up the r5r routing engine using the GTFS, elevation, OSM network, and study area data
r5r_network <- build_network(data_path = r5r_path, verbose = FALSE)

origins <- st_read("r5r/cambridge_origins.geojson")    # Hexagonal grid centroids
destinations <- st_read("r5r/cambridge_POIs.geojson")  # POIs points
departure_datetime <- as.POSIXct("13-05-2026 08:30:00", format = "%d-%m-%Y %H:%M:%S")

The accessibility measured used the hexagon centroids defined in Section 2.2 as origins, and the POIs defined in Section 2.1 as destinations, with a 20-minute threshold for walking, cycling and public transport modes. The accessibility considered all the origins and destinations within the study area and provided a deep analysis of the spatial accessibility across Cambridge and a standard mid-weekday in peak hour (08:30), to capture the maximum service frequency available.

3.2 Measuring accessibility

To measure the accessibility, the r5r::accessibility() function was used to calculate the number of opportunities (POIs) that can be reached within a 20-minute travel time threshold for each mode of transport in Cambridge. The following steps outline the process of calculating the accessibility scores for each mode and preparing the data for further analysis:

# Calculate accessibility from the hexagonal grid centroids to the POIs for both walking and PT modes
access_transit_r5r <- accessibility(
  r5r_network = r5r_network,
  origins = origins,    # The 100m hex centroids
  destinations = dest_wide,    # The widened POI table
  mode = c("WALK", "TRANSIT"),
  opportunities_col = c("Education", "Healthcare", "Supermarket", "Leisure"),
    decay_function = "step",    # Choose of decay function with AI assistance
  cutoffs = 20,    # 20-minute analysis
  departure_datetime = departure_datetime,
  n_threads = parallel::detectCores() - 1
)

The decay function used in the accessibility calculation was the “step” function, which applies a strict cutoff at the defined threshold (for the 20-minute accessibility analysis). Therefore, the accessibility function only considered the opportunities that can be reached within 20 minutes are counted as accessible. This choice was made to align with the 20-minute city concept, which emphasises the importance of having essential opportunities (services and amenities) within a strict time threshold for active travel and public transport modes.

3.3 Assumptions for the accessibility index

The accessibility scores calculated from the r5r::accessibility() function for each mode and POI type were normalised using a min-max normalisation approach (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, 2008) to scale the values between 0 and 1, allowing for a more standard comparison across the different modes and POI types.

\[ x_{norm} = \frac{x - x_{min}}{x_{max} - x_{min}} \]

This normalisation was defined as a function to be applied to each accessibility score, as detailed in the code chunk below.

# Define a normalisation function to scale the accessibility values between 0 and 1
norm <- function(x) {
  if(max(x, na.rm = TRUE) == min(x, na.rm = TRUE)) return(rep(0, length(x)))
  return ((x - min(x, na.rm = TRUE)) / (max(x, na.rm = TRUE) - min(x, na.rm = TRUE)))
}   # Assisted with AI to handle the case to avoid division by zero

The normalised accessibility scores for each POI type were then weighted equally (25% for each opportunity) to calculate a final accessibility score for each mode. Although, the weighting of the POI types could be adjusted based on the specific context and relevance of the opportunities (Büttner et al., 2024), an equal weighting was chosen to reflect the equal importance of access to education, healthcare, amenities, and leisure in the context of a 20-minute active travel city strategy (Krenn et al., 2015; Santos et al., 2022). The final accessibility score for each mode was calculated as a composite index.

Lastly, the accessibility was joined on a master dataset, as defined in the code chunk below, to combine the accessibility scores for all modes into a single dataset for further analysis and visualisation.

# Combine the accessibility scores for all modes into a single dataset
access_score <- transit_score |> 
  left_join(transit_score_30, by = "id") |> 
  left_join(walk_score, by = "id") |> 
  left_join(cycle_score, by = "id")

4 Results: Spatial accessibility

This section presents the results of the accessibility analysis across Cambridge, focusing on the spatial distribution of accessibility scores for active travel and public transport modes. The results are visualised through maps of the accessibility levels across the study area.

4.1 Active travel accessibility

The accessibility analysis for a 20-minute threshold for both active travel and public transport modes show the differences in the spatial distribution across Cambridge. The following figure (see Figure 2) shows the accessibility levels for these two modes. Moreover, the analysis reveals that the city centre of Cambridge has the highest accessibility levels, indifferent from the mode, which is expected given the concentration of opportunities and the radial structure of the public transport network (as shown in Figure 1).

There is also a difference of how the accessibility levels evolves from the urban area to the peri-urban areas of Cambridge. The accessibility levels of public transport show a concentrated pattern only around the city centre, while the accessibility levels for cycling show a more dispersed pattern across the urban area, where the peri-urban areas are still maintaining relatively high accessibility levels compared to the levels from public transport.

Finally, this difference can be attributed to the fact that active travel modes, such as cycling and walking, are less dependent on the spatial distribution of the network and can provide efficient and direct connections to opportunities across the city, only penalised from the topography of the city. On the other hand, public transport accessibility rely on the location of the stops, timetables, routes and the time required to access to the stops and destinations.

4.2 Public transport accessibility

To fully understand the accessibility levels for the public transport (from the combination of walking and transit modes), an analysis was conducted to compare the 20-minute and 30-minute accessibility scores for public transport mode across the study area. This approach isolates the effect of the time threshold on the public transport accessibility in Cambridge and provides insights into how the accessibility levels evolve when this parameter is modified.

The following figure (see Figure 3) illustrates the spatial comparison of the accessibility scores for both thresholds, showing how the accessibility levels change when the time threshold is increased.

The results indicate that the increase of the time threshold from 20 to 30 minutes significantly increases the accessibility levels across the city, especially in the peripheral areas. This suggests that a 20-minute threshold may be too restrictive for public transport accessibility in Cambridge, and that a more flexible threshold could provide a more accurate representation of the accessibility levels for this mode. Also, the analysis show that even the 30-minute threshold still maintains a significant accessibility gap between the public transport and the cycling modes, which highlights the potential of active travel modes to provide high accessibility levels across the city, even with strict time thresholds.

4.3 Identification of inaccessible areas

From the previous sections (see Section 4.1 and Section 4.2), it is seen that the accessibility levels for both active travel and public transport modes vary across Cambridge, with areas experiencing high accessibility and a radial pattern of how the accessibility is reduced towards the peri-urban area of the city. To further analyse this variation, an identification of the inaccessible areas for both modes (cycling and public transport) was conducted.

The inaccessibility areas are defined as the areas that have an accessibility score below a certain threshold (in this case, 25 out of 100) for both modes. This threshold was chosen to identify the areas that have very low accessibility levels and may require targeted interventions to improve accessibility. 6 Discussion and conclusions

6.1 Synthesis of findings

Spatial accessibility (RQ01): In response to the first research question, the analysis revealed the variation of accessibility levels across Cambridge. While active travel modes, primarily cycling, provide a resilient and efficient mode in the city, public transport accessibility remains limited, especially in the peripheral areas. The 20-minute threshold for public transport may be too restrictive, and a more flexible threshold could provide a more accurate representation of accessibility levels for this mode.

Spatial exclusion (RQ02): In response of the second research question, the inaccessible areas of Cambridge were identified, showing that a significant proportion of the population in the peripheral areas is excluded from having access to essential opportunities. Therefore, these areas do not provide an adequate 20-minute accessibility due several factor, such as the spatial distribution of opportunities, the quality of the infrastructure and the location of public transport stops and routes.

Socio-economic equity (RQ03): Finally, in response of the third research question, the analysis shows the positive correlation between the cycling accessibility level and the deprivation level, where the most deprived areas have the lowest accessibility levels. Furthermore, it presents a proportion of the estimated population that is excluded or lives near inaccessible areas, demonstrating the socio-economic inequity that Cambridge experience.

6.2 Methodological limitations

While the approach of this report provides a tool to evaluate the 20-minute accessibility levels in Cambridge, some methodological and data-related limitations must be acknowledged:

  • The accessibility level was calculated in a hexagonal 100 m grid and was aggregated up to the LSOA level, in order to join the results with the socio-economic variables. In this way, the accessibility level for a single LSOA may cover up a behavioral variation alongside each LSOA, with neighbourhoods with higher accessibility than others within the same LSOA.

  • The decay function used in the r5r package was defined as a 20-minute cutoff to evaluate the accessibility levels for different modes in Cambridge. However, this function may be too strict to evaluate the the accessibility. As a result, opportunities reachable in less than 20 minutes are considered accessible, but if one opportunity is reachable in 21 minutes or in a close range, it is considered inaccessible. It could be worth to analyse the other functions within the package as the r5r::travel_time_matrix() or r5r::isochrones().

  • The data regarding the public transport system is represented in the GTFS format that represent not only the network system, but the scheduled timetable defined for each route. In this way, the data consider a theoretical scenario and to improve the data reliability, it could be possible to add GPS data (also available at the BODS portal) to represent the delays and congestion of the system in Cambridge (Braga et al., 2023).

  • The cycle accessibility level considers the network of Cambridge alongside with the gradient from the topography. However, it lacks to consider the perceived safety and the traffic stress effect that make cyclist decide whether a route is safe and comfortable than other. For a further detailed analysis, it would be recommended to analyse the STATS19 accidents data and consider preferred routes of cyclists (Arellana et al., 2020).

6.3 Policy recommendations

The results presented in the report may have direct implications for transport planning policies and active travel development in Cambridge. As discussed in Section 4 and Section 5, the high accessibility levels are located in the urban city centre, while the peripheral areas (also the deprived areas) suffer from lack of this accessibility. Therefore, local authorities must prioritise to improve the spatial and socio-economic gap identified with data-driven interventions, such as bike share schemes, infrastructure and improvement of the schedules of the public transport network.
